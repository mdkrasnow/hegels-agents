### 1. Utilitarianism (Consequentialism)

**Core Principle:** Utilitarianism, a form of consequentialism (as mentioned in [Source 1]), posits that the most ethical choice is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number of people. It focuses on the outcomes or consequences of actions.

**Practical Strengths:**
*   **Focus on Outcomes:** It aligns with a common intuition that good decisions lead to positive results. This makes it highly practical for public policy, legislation, and resource allocation where the goal is to maximize overall societal well-being (e.g., healthcare policies, environmental regulations, economic decisions).
*   **Flexibility:** It can adapt to diverse situations by evaluating which action will yield the best consequences in that specific context, rather than adhering to rigid rules.
*   **Rational Calculation:** It encourages a systematic assessment of potential benefits and harms, which can be useful in complex scenarios involving many stakeholders.

**Practical Weaknesses:**
*   **Difficulty in Predicting Consequences:** In modern complex scenarios, predicting all long-term consequences of an action, especially across diverse populations, is incredibly challenging, if not impossible.
*   **Measurement Challenges:** Quantifying and comparing "happiness" or "utility" across different individuals and groups is subjective and difficult. How do we compare economic benefit with environmental harm, or individual suffering with collective gain?
*   **Potential for Injustice:** A strict utilitarian approach could, in theory, justify actions that harm a minority if it benefits a larger majority, raising concerns about individual rights and fairness. This can be problematic in modern diverse societies.
*   **Demanding Nature:** Constantly calculating the greatest good for the greatest number can be an overwhelming and impractical demand for everyday moral decisions.

### 2. Deontological Ethics

**Core Principle:** Deontological ethics focuses on duties, rules, and the inherent rightness or wrongness of actions themselves, irrespective of their consequences. It emphasizes moral obligations and universal principles.
