*   **Basis for Rights and Legal Systems:** By emphasizing "respect for human dignity and autonomy" (Source 2), deontology forms a strong foundation for human rights, legal frameworks, and professional codes of conduct. These systems rely on universal duties and rights that apply regardless of outcomes, providing clear guidance on what is permissible or impermissible. **Limitations in Practical Guidance:**
Despite its strengths, deontology can be less practical in complex modern dilemmas due to:
*   **Rigidity and Conflict of Duties:** Its rigidity "can lead to morally questionable outcomes" and "conflict between duties can be difficult to resolve" (Source 2). In situations where duties clash (e.g., the duty to tell the truth vs. the duty to protect an innocent life), deontology may not offer clear guidance on which duty takes precedence.

### 2. Utilitarianism: Practicality in Policy and Outcome-Based Decisions

**Practical Guidance:**
Utilitarianism, a form of consequentialism (Source 1), focuses on maximizing overall happiness or well-being.
*   **Policy Making and Resource Allocation:** It is highly practical in public policy, healthcare, and economic decision-making where the goal is to achieve the greatest good for the greatest number. For instance, public health policies, environmental regulations, or infrastructure projects often employ utilitarian reasoning to assess which option will yield the most benefit for the largest segment of the population.
*   **Outcome-Oriented Solutions:** It provides a clear metric for evaluation by focusing on consequences, prompting decision-makers to consider the broader impact of their actions.
